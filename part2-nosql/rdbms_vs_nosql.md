## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, I would recommend MySQL as the primary database. Healthcare systems deal with highly structured and sensitive information such as patient demographics, appointments, diagnoses, prescriptions, billing records, and insurance details. This kind of data requires strong consistency, reliability, and transactional safety. MySQL supports ACID properties, which means transactions are atomic, consistent, isolated, and durable. In a healthcare context, this is critical because errors such as duplicate prescriptions, lost billing entries, or inconsistent patient records can have serious consequences.

MongoDB, on the other hand, is more aligned with BASE-oriented systems where flexibility and availability are prioritized over strict consistency. It is very useful when the data model changes frequently or when documents have highly variable structures. However, for a core patient management system, the risk of inconsistency is a major concern. According to the CAP theorem, distributed systems often balance consistency, availability, and partition tolerance. In healthcare, consistency should be prioritized over eventual consistency for core transactional data.

That said, my answer would partly change if the startup also needed a fraud detection module. Fraud detection often involves high-volume, semi-structured, and fast-changing data such as activity logs, device metadata, claim patterns, or behavioral signals. In that case, MongoDB or another NoSQL system could be added as a secondary store for flexible ingestion and rapid analysis. So the best overall design would be a hybrid approach: MySQL for the core patient and billing system, and MongoDB for the fraud detection or analytics-related module. This provides both transactional integrity and flexibility where needed.
